Web of Deceit: Unraveling Digital Threats

Beneath Seattle's high-tech scene, there's a secret group doing tricky things online. They're skilled at using computers for sneaky stuff, like breaking into systems and clever money games. These folks also enjoy pretending to be someone else on the internet. A computer person might act like a digital artist, and a money person might turn into a secret detective. They create different online characters, blurring what's real and what's not. Their main focus is playing with money in a smart way. They use digital cash, like a secret money stream, making transactions that are hard to trace. It's like a hidden dance where they can make or lose a lot of money with just a few clicks.Even though Seattle's tech world is about new ideas, there's this secret group doing their own thing. Most people might not even know they exist, but they're like a hidden team playing their own game in the digital world. Identifying the hotspots of these digital hazards is critical for uncovering the core causes and paving the way for suitable risk mitigation strategies. The data used in this analysis was obtained from the Seattle Open Data portal and spans a wide range of 53 crime types from 2008 to the present.

The emphasis is on displaying data for the year 2023, notably focusing on 'Identity Theft,' 'Hacking/Computer Invasion,' and 'Wire Fraud' to highlight digital threat hotspots in Seattle. The visual representation of these risks in the Seattle area making use of folium map with the latitude and longitude of the region. Initializing the markers to show the location of the crime, modifying the markers' symbols for different crimes for accessibility, and adding the tooltip for additional information about the incident. The Map includes a filter button which enables you to sort the data by offense type. Instead of overloading the viewer with an abundance of individual markers, Marker Cluster will gather close markers into clusters with different colors dependent on the size indicating the hotspots. As the user zooms in, these clusters dynamically disperse, revealing individual markers for further investigation. The map as a whole retains readability and engagement, even when dealing with a large number of data points on the map.

Reference :

Seattle Crime Dataset(2008 - Present)
